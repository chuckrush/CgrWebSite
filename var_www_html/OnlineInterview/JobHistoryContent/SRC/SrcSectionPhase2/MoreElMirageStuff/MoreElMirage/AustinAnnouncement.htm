'Giant leap for unmanned kind'

Army Conducts First-Ever UAS Flight Using Just Ground-Based Radars

Inside the Pentagon -- The Army this week successfully completed the first-ever flight of an unmanned drone in U.S. airspace without using a chase plane or visual observer of some kind.

The first flight using the ground-based, sense-and-avoid system took off early Tuesday morning, nearly eight months after the Army received the initial go-ahead from the Federal Aviation Administration.

The Army intends to fly the unmanned aircraft system every night for two weeks (weather permitting) with intense FAA oversight to test the ground-based system, according to Viva Austin, the Army's product director for unmanned systems airspace integration concepts.

"It's just for us to make sure that the system works as we expect it to," Austin said.

She said the Army will be collecting data on the radar and weather conditions, we well as tracking how the operators respond to the display. This data will enable the Army to track what happens if other aircraft enter the airspace and how procedures are followed, Austin said. Each day the FAA and the Army will sit down and analyze the data together, she added.

The FAA oversight for the first 40 hours of flight was required in a detailed memorandum of agreement between the Pentagon and the FAA, which was signed last month. That agreement, which lays out what data the military department needs to collect, paved the way for the unmanned flights to get off the ground.

Last August, the FAA initially approved the Army's certificate of authorization, or COA, to fly the planes at El Mirage flight operations facility in California during the nighttime using just ground-based, sense-and-avoid technology. But the Army and the FAA disputed what data should be collected, finally reaching an agreement in early December. The Pentagon approved the data-collection requirements and signed the agreement in March, allowing the FAA to reissue the year-long COA on March 17.

After the first 40 hours of flight, the Army will provide summary reports to the FAA, sharing any potential anomalies or unexpected occurrences. The Army and General Atomics, the developer of the Grey Eagles being used under the COA, will test and train under this COA and fly as needed.

Austin said that the Army has "big plans to grow" this capability into one where the UAS can maneuver around other vehicles entering its airspace. Currently, the UAS must either return to restricted airspace or land if a plane enters its air volume. That capability will not fall under this COA, Austin said.

"This is the first-ever time that [ground-based sense and avoid] has been done, so we started small," Austin said. "We've already started on growing that capability, but for now we're just going to enjoy this moment and kind of bask in the glory so to speak."

"It's one small step for the Army, but I guess, to paraphrase Neil Armstrong, one giant leap for unmanned kind," she added.

In an April 22 interview prior to the first flight, Austin said the first night of flying was being treated much like a shuttle launch. "Everything will have to be absolutely perfect; we'll make sure it's very safe, so if anything goes wrong, we may push it hours or a day or whatever just to make sure everything is right for the first try," Austin said. The flight went as planned, however.

This first flight required "the stars to align," Austin said. The Army and FAA had to line up travel schedules, but the potential of furloughs and a government shutdown due to the lack of a fiscal year 2011 appropriations bill created challenges. In addition, General Atomics had to work to get its crews trained and available at night.

She said that the program came together after support from a number of different people and programs, including everyone in the UAS Project Manger's Office, the Grey Eagle program manager, the modernization program manager, General Atomics and the Pentagon's UAS Task Force. Austin also praised the FAA.

"They are sending crews out there; they've worked with our time lines; they're going to stay up all night with us," Austin said. "We really couldn't do it without them because they have to be part of these first flights." -- Jordana Mishory
